Part 1 (200 words).

1. Describe your potential educational context: What level is this text for? For a group for an individual lesson? Student age? Needs?

This lesson is designed for a group of adult learners at a B2 level. The group consists of 8–10 professionals who frequently experience workplace pressure and are interested in psychology and self-improvement. Their primary needs include developing listening fluency for natural, fast-paced speech and expanding their vocabulary related to emotions and physiological responses.

2. Explain why you have selected this material for this particular group/student:

●    Why do you think this text is appropriate for your group/student? (refer to their level, text type/genre and length)

●    Why would the text you have chosen interest your learner/group of learners?

●    How would specific learners’ needs in skills development be catered for in this lesson?

 

This material is appropriate for B2 learners because it features a clear but authentic rhetorical style. The topic of stress management is of high general interest, providing a strong hook for discussion and personal reflection. The text caters to skills development by offering opportunities to practice "listening for gist" (understanding the core "reset" concept) and "listening for detail" (identifying specific physiological techniques).

 

Part 2 (800 words)

1. How would you generate interest in the topic of the text?  Why is this important?

To generate interest in Jenny Taitz’s TED Talk on "Stress Resets," we would display three uncaptioned images: a person splashing ice-cold water on their face, someone exhaling deeply with eyes closed, and a professional looking overwhelmed at a desk. Then we would ask students in pairs to guess the connection and predict what "hack" links them. This approach activates schemata by triggering background knowledge and personal experiences with stress, making the content easier to process. It increases motivation by creating a "need to know" through a puzzle rather than a vocabulary list. It lowers the affective filter with a low-stakes discussion about a universal experience, reducing anxiety often caused by authentic materials. Finally, it establishes purpose by setting a clear context and showing real-life relevance.

2. What pre-reading/pre-listening activities will you do to prepare your learners for your text? Which items of vocabulary from the text would you pre-teach and why? (Not how!)

Following the lead-in, we would pre-teach the following five items of blocking vocabulary that are essential for understanding the key content of the talk: overwhelmed, to ruminate, a reset (in the psychological sense), and resilience. These terms are central to the speaker's argument and recur throughout the talk. Without understanding them, students would be unable to complete either the gist or the detail task successfully. The group is a mixed-ability one, so while some learners may know words like 'overwhelmed', others may not, and items such as 'to ruminate' are likely to be unfamiliar even at B2 level.

3. Design and describe the first reading/listening task, stating the target sub-skill (gist or specific information).  Attach any materials(e.g., handouts with your questions and the text) you would distribute (and include an answer key).  Explain clearly how the task will help your learners practise the target sub-skill.  Describe the feedback you would do.

Task 1. Listening 1 for gist

Target sub-skill: Listening for gist (global understanding / identifying the speaker's main message and stance).

Task: Students listen to the TED Talk once and answer the single gist question: "Does Jenny Taitz think we can overcome stress completely, or does she think we can learn to manage it better? Support your answer with one example from the talk."

Answer key: She believes we can learn to manage stress better but not eliminate it completely.

Taitz states that stress is inevitable and is the price of a meaningful life. She presents practical 'resets' as coping tools rather than cures. Quotes from the video to support the idea: "If you can create stress, you can also learn to reset it in minutes. No long meditations, medications or martinis required. Just shifts in your mind, body and behavior", "Stress is less about what you're facing and more about believing you can cope."

 

Listening for gist requires students to identify the speaker's overall stance and message without focusing on every word. This sub-skill involves listening 'for the overall picture' rather than every detail (Harmer, 2007). The single open question encourages students to listen actively and globally rather than scanning for specific phrases, which is a different cognitive process from detail listening. The requirement to give a supporting example stops students from guessing and ensures they have engaged with the content.

 

Feedback: After listening, students discuss their answers in pairs for 1–2 minutes. I then conduct whole-class feedback, asking 2–3 students to share their answers and their supporting evidence, facilitating brief discussion of any disagreements.

Task 2. Listening 2 for detail

Target sub-skill: Listening for detail (understanding specific information from the text).

Task: Students listen to the talk for a second time and answer the following questions:

1. "According to Jenny Taitz, what is stress?"

(In the video stress is described like this: "When what we're facing feels like too much, spilled milk becomes a flood, a headache at work spills into heartache at home. Stress doesn't just happen. It's something we easily co-create, then spread like the flu")

2. "What are the three types of resets she describes?"

(Mind resets, body resets, and behaviour resets. Taitz explicitly divides her strategies into these three categories throughout the talk)

3. "When would you need a mind reset?"

(When you are stuck in overthinking, imagining worst-case scenarios, having repetitive negative thoughts)

4. Give ONE example of a body reset from the talk.

(Making a half-smile. Quote from the video: "No need to freeze your forehead. Half-smiling is your free and natural version. Try being miserable or battling road rage with a resting Buddha face")

5. "What is an example of a behaviour reset?"

(Acting opposite of how you feel. Noticing your emotion-driven urge. Quote from the video: "ask yourself if acting on it is ultimately helpful. If not, do the opposite")

 

These questions require students to listen attentively to specific parts of the talk and locate precise information, which is the essence of listening for detail. This sub-skill involves students focusing carefully on the text in order to extract particular facts or ideas. The questions are paraphrased so that students cannot simply match words from the question to the talk; they must demonstrate genuine understanding. The variety of question types (definition, list, exemplification) covers different sections of the talk.

 

Feedback: Students first compare answers in pairs. Then go through the answers with the whole class, replaying the relevant sections of the talk for any items that were unclear or produced disagreement.

5. Design and describe the follow-up production task (speaking or writing) – attach any materials (handouts) you would distribute.  Explain how this task will help your learners practise the target skill (not language).  Describe how you would set the activity and how you would organize feedback.

Task 3. Speaking (production)

The follow-up speaking task will be a structured discussion in pairs or small groups of three, based on the following questions provided on a handout:

1. "Do you think stress is always a bad thing? Why or why not?"

2. "Which of the three types of resets (mind, body, behaviour) do you find most useful? Why?"

3. "What do you usually do when you feel overwhelmed? Does it help?"

4. "Is it easy to talk about stress with friends or family in your culture? Why?"

5. "What advice would you give a friend who is really struggling with stress at university?"

 

The questions are personalised and opinion-based, which means that no student is 'wrong' and all students have something to contribute regardless of ability. The task also builds naturally on the content of the TED Talk, meaning that the listening input has served as a springboard for extended speaking, which increases the cohesion and communicative purpose of the lesson as a whole (Scrivener, 2011).

 

Setting the activity: We would give each student a copy of the discussion questions, allow one minute for students to read and think, then assign pairs or groups of three. Students discuss for approximately 10–12 minutes.

Feedback: After the pair/group work, I invite each pair to share one insight from their discussion with the whole class, either something they agreed on, something surprising, or their most useful personal strategy. Avoid correcting language during the activity itself but note down 3–4 recurring errors for brief delayed feedback at the end.

1. How to Teach English, J. Harmer, Pearson Education Limited, 2007.

2. Learning Teaching, J. Scrivener, 3rd Edition, Macmillan Education, 2011.
