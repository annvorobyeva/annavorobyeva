Grammar point: Intermediate Gerunds & Infinitives

Target verbs:

  • Verb + V-ing: like, love, hate, enjoy, can't stand
  • Verb + to + inf: decide, promise, offer, expect

 

1. Controlled Practice task: "Choose the correct form"

 

Task instructions:

Part A: Circle the correct option to complete each sentence.

 

1. My brother can't stand (to wait / waiting) in long queues at the supermarket.

2. The manager offered (to give / giving) us a discount on our next purchase.

3. We expect (to arrive / arriving) at the airport at least two hours before the flight.

4. Sarah enjoys (to paint / painting) landscapes in her free time.

5. Tom decided (to quit / quitting) his job and travel the world.

6. I promised (to help / helping) my neighbour with her garden this weekend.

 

Part B: Put the words in the correct order to form a logical sentence.

 

1. (to / promised / has / the project / She / finish) by Friday.

____________________________________________________.

1. (stand / people / I / loudly / can't / in / eat / cinemas / who)

____________________________________________________.

1. (expect / to / we / receive / Do / the package / tomorrow?)

____________________________________________________?

 

Teacher's role: Monitor closely. After students finish, check answers as a class. For Part B, ask different students to write their answers on the board to check word order and spelling.

 

Keys:

Part A: Multiple choice

1. waiting – After "can't stand," use the gerund (verb + -ing).

2. to give – After "offer," use the infinitive (to + verb).

3. to arrive – After "expect," use the infinitive (to + verb).

4. painting – After "enjoy," use the gerund (verb + -ing).

5. to quit – After "decide," use the infinitive (to + verb).

6. to help – After "promise," use the infinitive (to + verb).

 

Part B: Sentence reordering

1. She has promised to finish the project by Friday.

Structure: has + promised + to + finish

2. I can't stand people who eat loudly in cinemas.

Alternative word order: I can't stand people who eat in cinemas loudly.

Structure: can't stand + [noun phrase] + who + [verb]

3. Do we expect to receive the package tomorrow?

Structure: Do + we + expect + to + receive

 

 

2. Semi-Controlled Practice task: "Find the error - advice column"

 

Part A

Task instruction: Read this letter from a teenager to an advice columnist. There are 6 mistakes in the use of gerunds and infinitives. Find the mistakes, correct them, and then write two sentences of advice using the target verbs.

 

Handout:

 

Dear Agony Aunt,

I'm really frustrated with my best friend, Max. Last week, he promised helping me study for my math test, but he never showed up. He just decided go to the cinema with his other friends instead. I really enjoy to spend time with him usually, but lately I can't stand to be around him. He always offers paying for things, but then he expects me to lend him money the next day! What should I do?

Signed,

Confused in Cambridge

 

Keys:

1. promised helping → promised to help

2. decided go → decided to go

3. enjoy to spend → enjoy spending

4. can't stand to be → can't stand being

5. offers paying → offers to pay

6. (No mistake in "expects me to lend" - this one is correct!)

 

Part B

  • Now it's your turn: Write two sentences giving advice to "Confused in Cambridge." Use at least two of the target verbs in your advice (like, love, hate, enjoy, can't stand; decide, promise, offer, expect)

 

    1. ________________________________________________________________.

    2. ________________________________________________________________.

 

Teacher's Role: Walk around and check that students are identifying the correct errors. Encourage them to explain why the form is wrong. When they write their own advice sentences, ensure they are using the target verbs accurately.

 

Keys:

Part B: Advice sentences (Sample answers)

Note: Student answers will vary. Below are examples of correct sentences using the target verbs.

    1. 1. "You should decide to talk to Max honestly about how you feel."

    2. 2. "I expect Max to apologise for breaking his promises."

    3. 3. "Next time, don't expect him to help if he keeps letting you down."

    4. 4. "If he offers to pay for something, suggest doing an activity that doesn't cost money."

    5. 5. "Try to enjoy spending time with other friends who respect you."

    6. 6. "You can't stand being treated badly, so set clear boundaries with him."

    7. 7. "He promised to help you, so you have the right to be upset."

    8. 8. "Maybe you should decide to give him one more chance, but be careful."

 

 

3. Freer Practice task: Role-play – inviting someone

 

Instructions:

 

Imagine the situation: It's Friday afternoon. Student A invites Student B to do something this weekend. Student B accepts or refuses.

 

Student A – You invite

  • Greet your friend
  • Ask about their weekend plans
  • Invite them to do something
  • If they say yes, arrange a time
  • If they say no, say "maybe next time"

Student B – You reply

  • Greet your friend
  • Answer questions about your weekend
  • Accept or refuse the invitation
  • If you accept, suggest a time
  • If you refuse, give a simple reason

 

Useful language for the role-play:

Inviting

Accepting

Refusing

"Do you want to...?"

"Sure! I'd love to."

"Sorry, I can't. I promised to..."

"Would you like to...?"

"That sounds great!"

"I'd love to, but I expect to..."

"I decided to... Do you want to join?"

"Yes! I enjoy doing that."

"Maybe another time. I hate doing that on weekends."

"I promised myself to... Fancy coming?"

"Why not? I love that!"

"No thanks, I can't stand that."

 

Example Dialogue

Student A: "Hi! What do you expect to do this weekend?"

Student B: "Not much. I promised to help my mum with cleaning."

Student A: "Oh. Well, I decided to go to the cinema on Saturday. Do you want to come?"

Student B: "I'd love to, but I can't. I promised to help my mum. Maybe Sunday?"

Student A: "Sunday works. What time?"

Student B: "How about 3 pm? I enjoy watching films in the afternoon."

Student A: "Perfect! See you Sunday."

 

There are no keys, as the answers of students will be unique.
