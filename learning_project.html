Participants: Vorobyova Anna, Demicheva Diana

Teacher's Project Plan

Step 1: Learners group

●      Age: 16–17 years old (11th grade).

●      Level: Intermediate (B1).

●      Number of students: 10–12 students.

●      Interests: This group is highly motivated by digital trends, technology, social media, and environmental sustainability. They are preparing for university entrance and international English exams, meaning they need to practice structured argumentative speaking and real-world problem-solving.

 

Step 2: Project Theme, task, and final product

Theme: "Smart and green cities of the future."

Task: In pairs, students will research an existing urban issue (e.g., traffic pollution, waste management, or lack of green spaces) and propose a technology-driven or eco-friendly solution for their own city.

Final product: A digital multimedia pitch (a presentation with 5–6 slides or a short video) mapping out their innovation.

Rationale: This theme directly taps into their interest in tech and sustainability while preparing them for high-stakes exam tasks that require evaluating complex global issues, expressing structured opinions, and balancing pros and cons.

 

Step 3: Learning outcomes

Our students will be able to collaborate in pairs to research and evaluate environmental/technological problems, organize an argumentative presentation using a clear structure (problem, solution, advantages, disadvantages). Also, students will be able to use advanced transition words and cause-and-effect vocabulary (e.g., consequently, as a result, leads to) to present a persuasive idea, as well as handle follow-up questions from an audience spontaneously.

 

Step 4: Assessment criteria

Content: Originality of the urban solution and clear identification of the problem.

Organization, structure: Logical flow following the designated presentation template.

Language production: Correct use of target vocabulary (urban/eco terms) and linking devices; delivery skills (speaking naturally rather than reading text).

Interaction: Ability to answer peer questions clearly and ask meaningful questions during other pitches.

 

Step 5: Required equipment

●      Laptops, tablets, or smartphones with internet access for research.

●      Presentation software (e.g., Canva, Google Slides, or PowerPoint).

●      A projector or smart screen for the final presentation day.

●      Online collaboration tools (e.g., Google Docs or Padlet) for shared brainstorming notes.

 

Step 6: Student support

●      Vocabulary pre-teaching: A vocabulary matching worksheet focused on smart city features and green tech (e.g., renewable energy, infrastructure, public transit, carbon footprint).

●      Language frames: A sentence starter bank for highlighting cause/effect and pros/cons (e.g., Implementing this will result in..., While it reduces cost, a major drawback is...).

●      Structural templates: A digital slide layout guide ensuring students know exactly what content belongs on each slide.

●      Presentation tips: A mini-lesson focusing on maintaining eye contact, using visual cues, and pacing.

 

Step 7:Project procedure

To introduce the project, we will present a 3-minute model pitch titled "Green roofs for cleaner air."

 

1. Introduction: We will model introducing myself and stating the city-wide problem (urban heat islands and low air quality).

2. The proposal: We will present the solution (mandatory rooftop gardens on public buildings) using clear visuals.

3. Pros & cons analysis: We'll highlight language structures by listing two distinct benefits (insulation, air filtration) and one major drawback (high initial installation costs), emphasizing signposting phrases like "On the flip side..."

4. Conclusion & review: After concluding, we will pass out a printed copy of the model slides. Together, the class will color-code the language functions: yellow for the problem statement, green for the advantages, and pink for the contrasting drawbacks.
